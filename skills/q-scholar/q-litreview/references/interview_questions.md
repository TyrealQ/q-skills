# Literature Review Interview Protocol

Use these questions to gather the information needed to draft a standalone literature review section. Adapt questions based on the research context and user's existing materials.

**Important:** If an introduction already exists, read it first. The literature review should elaborate on what the introduction previews, not re-introduce the same material at the same level of detail.

## 1. Existing Introduction and Cross-Section Scope

### 1.1 Introduction Status
- Does a standalone introduction already exist?
- What literature does the introduction preview? (List the studies and streams it mentions)
- How does the introduction characterize the theoretical framework? (Brief reference vs. detailed definition)
- What gaps does the introduction identify?

### 1.2 Cross-Section Coordination
- Which studies should be elaborated in the lit review that the intro only mentions parenthetically?
- Are there concrete illustrative examples in the intro that should be moved to the lit review?
- Does the intro state the RQs? If so, how should the lit review arrive at them differently?

## 2. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 Primary Theory
- What is the primary theoretical framework?
- What are its core constructs or dimensions?
- What foundational studies established this framework?
- What mechanisms does the framework propose (e.g., how do constructs relate to outcomes)?

### 2.2 Extensions and Applications
- How has the framework been extended beyond its original context?
- What computational or digital applications have been developed?
- What specific studies demonstrated the framework's applicability to new domains?

### 2.3 Domain Application
- How does the framework apply to the study's specific domain?
- What is distinctive about how the domain relates to the framework's constructs?
- What existing domain-specific frameworks exist, and what do they capture vs. miss?

### 2.4 Gap
- Where has the framework not yet been applied?
- Why is this gap consequential (what makes this context the one where the framework is most needed)?

## 3. Prior Research Tradition

### 3.1 Foundational Research
- What is the field's core research tradition relevant to this study?
- What are the foundational studies and their specific findings?
- What measurement instruments or methodological approaches dominate?

### 3.2 Extensions
- How has this research tradition been extended to new settings or populations?
- What specific studies extended the tradition, and what did they find?

### 3.3 Complementary Frameworks
- What complementary theories or frameworks have been applied?
- What do they add that the core tradition alone cannot capture?

### 3.4 Methodological Pattern
- What methodological orientation characterizes the field (e.g., survey-based, experimental, observational)?
- What does this orientation capture well?
- What does it leave unaddressed?

## 4. Environmental/Platform Dynamics

### 4.1 Context Shift
- How has the environment (platform, market, technology) restructured the phenomenon?
- What new dynamics does the current environment introduce?

### 4.2 Content and Actor Diversity
- What types of content or actors operate in this environment?
- How do they vary in characteristics, production approaches, or reach?

### 4.3 Outcome Metrics
- What observable outcome metrics are available?
- How do different metrics capture different forms of response?

## 5. Limitations and New Approach

### 5.1 Instrument Limitations
- What can existing measurement instruments not capture about the phenomenon?
- What properties of the content or environment are invisible to current tools?

### 5.2 Analytical Limitations
- What analytical approaches dominate the field?
- What assumptions do they make that may not hold in this context?
- What types of complexity (combinatorial, configurational, nonlinear) do they miss?

### 5.3 The Study's Approach
- How does the study's approach address these limitations?
- What does it examine that prior work could not?
- How do the research questions emerge from these limitations?

## 6. Research Questions

- What are the research questions?
- How does each question map to the gaps and limitations identified above?
- How should the lit review arrive at these questions differently than the introduction states them?

## Interview Summary Template

After completing the interview, summarize responses:

```
## Existing Introduction
[What it covers, what it previews, cross-section coordination notes]

## Theoretical Framework
Primary: [Theory] - core constructs, foundational studies
Extensions: [Key computational/digital/domain applications]
Gap: [Where it has not been applied, why it matters]

## Prior Research Tradition
Core: [Foundational studies and findings]
Extensions: [How tradition extended to new settings]
Methodological pattern: [What it captures, what it misses]

## Environmental Dynamics
[How the environment restructures the phenomenon]
[Content/actor diversity, outcome metrics]

## Limitations
Instrument: [What current tools miss]
Analytical: [What current methods cannot capture]

## New Approach
[How the study addresses limitations]

## Research Questions (earned framing)
RQ1: [Question as consequence of the argument]
RQ2: [Question as consequence of the argument]
```

## Refinement Diagnostic

When the user has an existing literature review draft, use these questions to identify structural issues:

**Progressive Argument**
- Does the argument trace theory, application, limitations, new approach, RQs?
- Does each paragraph open by extending or complicating the prior paragraph's conclusion?
- Do the RQs feel earned by the argument, or are they restated from the introduction?

**Subsection Architecture**
- Does Subsection 1 establish the theory and close with the domain gap?
- Does Subsection 2 review the field's tradition and pivot to the new approach?
- Are the subsection headers descriptive (naming dimensions/activities, not just objects)?

**Cross-Section Coordination**
- Does the intro compress its literature treatment while the lit review elaborates?
- Is theory defined in full in the lit review and referenced briefly in the intro?
- Do illustrative examples appear in one section only?
- Does RQ framing differ between the two sections?

**Scope**
- Is the section purely conceptual and theoretical?
- Are there measurement methodology or analytical procedure details that belong in methods?

**Prose Quality**
- Are there redundant sentences (two sentences making the same point)?
- Are specific study findings woven into narrative, or are studies merely cataloged?
- Is citation density adequate (5-10 per claim where warranted)?
- Are there standalone intro paragraphs that merely announce what follows?
